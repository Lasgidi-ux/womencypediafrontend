María Sabina

Mazatec Curandera, Spiritual Healer, Keeper of Sacred Knowledge

Born

c. 1533 CE

Zazzau (modern-day Zaria, Nigeria)

Died

c. 1610 CE

Reign

Dynasty

Hausa Kingdom

María Sabina (1894–1985) was a Mazatec curandera (healer) from Huautla de Jiménez, Oaxaca, Mexico, who became internationally known for her use of psilocybin mushrooms in traditional healing ceremonies called veladas. She is regarded as one of the most important figures in the preservation of indigenous Mesoamerican spiritual practices. This biography is crafted through an interpretive lens that honors cultural context, symbolic power, lived experience, historical significance, and community memory.

Early Life and Calling

María Sabina Magdalena García was born on July 22, 1894, in Huautla de Jiménez, a remote mountain town in the Sierra Mazateca of Oaxaca, Mexico. She was born into a family of curanderos (healers); both her grandfather and great-grandfather were known practitioners of the sacred mushroom ceremonies.[1]

Orphaned at a young age after her father's death, María Sabina grew up in poverty, working to help support her family. According to her own account, she first consumed the sacred mushrooms (nti si tho, "little saints that spring forth") at the age of seven or eight, along with her sister María Ana, when they stumbled upon them while tending to their family's crops. She described the experience as profoundly spiritual, claiming that the mushrooms spoke to her and revealed healing knowledge.[2]

"There is a world beyond ours, a world that is far away, nearby, and invisible. And there is where God lives, where the dead live, the spirits and the saints, a world where everything has already happened and everything is known."— María Sabina

The Velada: Sacred Healing Practice

María Sabina's healing practice centered on the velada, an all-night ceremonial session in which she would ingest psilocybin mushrooms (primarily Psilocybe caerulescens and Psilocybe mexicana) and enter a trance state to diagnose and treat illness. The ceremonies combined prayer, chanting, rhythmic clapping, and the ingestion of mushrooms in a darkened room.[3]

During the velada, María Sabina would chant elaborate poetic verses known as cantos, which blended Catholic imagery with pre-Columbian Mazatec cosmology. Her chants invoked Christ, the Virgin Mary, and various Catholic saints alongside Mazatec spiritual entities. These chants have been recognized by scholars and poets as remarkable works of oral literature.[4]

She viewed the mushrooms not as drugs but as sacred intermediaries between the human and divine worlds. She used them strictly for healing purposes — to cure physical ailments, resolve emotional distress, find lost objects, and divine the future. Her practice was deeply embedded in the Mazatec worldview, where illness was understood as a spiritual as well as physical condition.

Encounter with the Western World

In 1955, American ethnomycologist R. Gordon Wasson and photographer Allan Richardson traveled to Huautla de Jiménez and participated in one of María Sabina's veladas. Wasson published an account of the experience in Life magazine in 1957, titled "Seeking the Magic Mushroom," which introduced psilocybin mushrooms to Western audiences for the first time.[5]

The publication had profound and largely negative consequences for María Sabina and her community. Thousands of Westerners — hippies, researchers, artists, and thrill-seekers — descended upon Huautla de Jiménez hoping to experience the mushrooms. Notable visitors included Timothy Leary, Bob Dylan, John Lennon, and Aldous Huxley, among others.

María Sabina later expressed deep regret about sharing the sacred ceremonies with outsiders. She believed that the foreigners' recreational use of the mushrooms desecrated their spiritual purpose and diminished their healing power. Her community blamed her for the disruption, and she was ostracized. Her house was burned down, and she spent her final years in poverty.[6]

"Before Wasson, I felt that the saint children elevated me. I don't feel like that anymore. The force has been diminished. If Cayetano García had not brought the foreigners… the saint children would have kept their power."— María Sabina, as recorded by Álvaro Estrada

Cultural Significance and Legacy

Despite the personal suffering she endured, María Sabina's legacy is immense. She is recognized as having single-handedly preserved and transmitted ancient Mazatec spiritual knowledge to a global audience. Her ceremonial chants, documented by researchers including Álvaro Estrada and Henry Munn, are considered masterpieces of indigenous oral literature.[7]

Octavio Paz, the Nobel Prize-winning Mexican poet, compared her chants to the great works of world poetry. Her verses demonstrate a sophisticated cosmological vision and an extraordinary capacity for spontaneous poetic composition during altered states of consciousness.

In Mexico, she has become a symbol of indigenous wisdom and cultural resilience. Her image appears on murals throughout Oaxaca, and she is honored as a cultural icon by indigenous rights movements across Latin America. The Mexican government posthumously recognized her contribution to the preservation of intangible cultural heritage.

Death and Commemoration

María Sabina died on November 22, 1985, in Huautla de Jiménez at the age of 91, living in poverty. Despite the global fame her ceremonies had brought, she received no financial benefit from the international interest in psilocybin mushrooms.

Today, she is commemorated through numerous cultural works, including Álvaro Estrada's biography María Sabina: Her Life and Chants (1977), documentary films, and artistic tributes. The Mazatec community in Huautla de Jiménez has reclaimed her legacy, and she is honored as one of the most important women in Mexican indigenous history. In 2019, the Mexican Senate paid tribute to her as a symbol of resistance and indigenous knowledge.
